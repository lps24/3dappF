History

Welcome to the History of the Coca-Cola Company

1880s

Like many people who change history, John Pemberton, an Atlanta pharmacist, was inspired by simple curiosity. One afternoon, he stirred up a fragrant, caramel-coloured liquid and, when it was done, he carried it a few doors down to Jacobs’ Pharmacy. Here the mixture was combined with carbonated water and sampled by customers who all agreed this new drink was something special. So Jacobs’ Pharmacy put it on sale for five cents (about €0.04) a glass.

1890s

The Coca‑Cola Spencerian script trademark is registered with the U.S. Patent office. The first building is erected for the sole purpose of housing The Coca‑Cola Company. It is quickly outgrown as the Company moves to larger quarters five times in the next 12 years.

1900s

The second bottling plant to begin production under the 1899 contract opens in Atlanta. The first advertising for Coca‑Cola appears in national magazines. Bottling operations begin in Canada, Cuba and Panama, becoming the first three countries outside the United States to bottle Coca‑Cola.

1910s

Answering the call of The Coca‑Cola Company, the contour bottle prototype is designed by Alexander Samuelson and patented by the Root Glass Company. It is approved by the Bottlers’ Association and becomes the standard bottle. The first bottling plants are opened in Europe in Paris and Bordeaux.

1920s

The introduction of the first six-bottle carton is a significant innovation for the beverage industry. The carton is patented the following year. Two significant innovations debut: the bell-shaped Coca‑Cola fountain glass and the first standardized open-top cooler.

1930s

Seeking to create an advertising program that links Coca‑Cola with Christmas, artist Haddon Sundbloom creates his first illustration showin Santa Claus pausing for a Coke. For the next three decades, from 1931 to 1964, Sundblom paints images of Santa that help to create the modern interpretation of St. Nick.

1940s

The Sprite Boy character is introduced in advertising to convey the message that “Coca‑Cola” and “Coke” are two terms that reference the same product. “Coke” becomes a registered trademark of The Coca‑Cola Company.

1950s

Coca‑Cola becomes the first product to appear on the cover of Time magazine. The first king-size bottles make their appearance in the United States. Fanta Orange is introduced in Naples, Italy, the first new product to be distributed by the Company. The Fanta line of flavored beverages comes to the United States in 1960.

1960s

Sprite, a lemon-lime beverage, is introduced on February 1 1963 TaB, the first diet drink produced by the company, is launched. Its name is selected from a computer-generated search that yields more than + 300,000 options. A new graphic look for the Coca‑Cola system is introduced, featuring a red-and-white color scheme and logo.

1970s

The 2-liter polyethylene terephthalate (PET) bottle is introduced, beginning the Company’s use of PET packaging. The North Avenue Tower headquarters building is first occupied. Coca‑Cola begins worldwide sponsorship of Special Olympics.

1980s

A Coca‑Cola contour bottle becomes the central focus of the movie “The Gods Must Be Crazy.” Bottling operations begin in Russia. Coca‑Cola becomes the first soft drink consumed in space when the astronauts aboard the space shuttle Challenger tested the space can on a mission.

1990s

As the Berlin Wall comes down, Coca‑Cola is sold in East Germany for the first time. The Centennial Olympic Games are held in Atlanta. The Coca‑Cola Company creates Coca‑Cola Olympic. The Coca‑Cola Company acquires Schweppes beverages in many markets around the world.

2000s

TThe Coca‑Cola Company sponsors the Olympic Games in Sydney, Australia. Simply Orange, a not-from-concentrate orange juice, is introduced. Coca‑Cola Zero, a zero-calorie cola, makes its debut.

2010s

The Coca‑Cola Company celebrates 125 years of brand Coca‑Cola. A Coca‑Cola Facebook page is established by two fans. The site has over 22 million fans worldwide as of January 2011 and continues to grow.
